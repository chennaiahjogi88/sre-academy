Blameless Culture

Core Principle: Systems Thinking

"The system allowed this to happen." Not: "Bob broke production."

Example: If one person can take down production, the system failed — not the person.

Why Blameless?

  • Psychological safety — people report issues honestly instead of hiding them
  • Honesty leads to better postmortems — people don't lie to protect themselves
  • Root cause analysis improves — focuses on systems, not people
  • Retention — people don't quit after incidents if blamed

Google's Philosophy

"We don't call it a failure — it's a learning opportunity. We don't blame people — we improve systems."

When to Write a Postmortem

Hard Rules

  • SEV1 incidents: Always write postmortem
  • Any SLO breach: Write postmortem
  • Customer-visible outage: Write postmortem

Grey Area (Use Judgment)

  • SEV2: Usually write, unless obvious and trivial fix
  • Near-misses: Even if not customer-facing, if could have been bad, write it
  • Security issues: Always write, even if contained

Timeline

Write within 24-48 hours of incident resolution. While memory is fresh but emotions have cooled.

Postmortem Template

Essential Sections

  • Title: "Database Connection Pool Exhaustion — Payment Service — Jan 15, 2026"
  • Date & Authors: Who wrote it, when
  • Status: Draft | Complete (incomplete PMRs are useless)
  • Summary (1 paragraph): What happened in plain English
  • Impact: How many customers, how long, $$ if applicable
  • Timeline: 13:42 - Alert fired | 13:47 - IC declared SEV1 | etc.
  • Root Cause: The actual reason (systems-level)
  • Contributing Factors: Things that made it worse
  • Action Items: Prevention (prevent recurring) + Detection (catch faster)
  • Lessons Learned: What we learned

The 5 Whys: Root Cause Analysis

How It Works

Ask "Why?" five times. Each answer leads to next "Why?". Uncovers system-level root cause.

Symptom: Payment service down
Why 1: All database connections exhausted
Why 2: Connections not being released after queries
Why 3: Connection pool timeout too long (30 min default)
Why 4: No monitoring alert on connection pool utilization
Why 5: Team assumed connection pool never fills up (incorrect assumption)
Root Cause: No alert monitoring connection pool health + assumption of unlimited connections

Action Items: SMART & Categorized

Action Item Requirements: SMART

  • Specific — "Add alert" not "improve monitoring"
  • Measurable — Know when it's done
  • Assigned — To a named person
  • Reasonable — Can be done within a month
  • Timed — Due date

Action Item Categories

Prevention: Stop this from happening again
E.g., "Fix connection pool timeout to 5 min" — Due Jan 20
Detection: Catch faster if it happens
E.g., "Add alert when connection pool > 80%" — Due Jan 18
Response: Respond faster
E.g., "Document connection pool troubleshooting runbook" — Due Jan 22
Process: Better systems/procedures
E.g., "Add connection pool review to architecture design checklist" — Due Jan 20

Postmortem Review Meeting

Format

  • Duration: 30 minutes max
  • Attendees: Incident participants + team leads + optional guests
  • Facilitator: Usually not the author (outside view)
  • Agenda: Summarize what happened, discuss root cause, review action items, no blame

Discussion Points

  • "Is the root cause correct? Are we addressing systems or blaming people?"
  • "Are action items SMART? Can we actually do them?"
  • "What surprised us? What did we learn?"
  • "Should we increase monitoring/alerting in other areas?"

After Meeting

  • Share postmortem widely (Slack, wiki, email)
  • Track action items in Jira with due dates
  • Follow up: In 30 days, did we complete action items?
  • If incident repeats, postmortem becomes "why did we miss this?"

Quiz: Postmortems & RCA

Q1: What is the core principle of blameless postmortems?
A: Systems thinking — "The system allowed this to happen," not blaming individuals. Focus on improving processes, not punishing people.
Q2: When should you write a postmortem?
A: Always for SEV1, for any SLO breach, for customer-visible outages. Usually for SEV2. Within 24-48 hours of resolution.
Q3: What is the goal of the 5 Whys technique?
A: To uncover the root cause at the systems level by asking "Why?" repeatedly. Moves from symptoms to systemic improvements.
Q4: What does SMART stand for in action items?
A: Specific, Measurable, Assigned, Reasonable, Timed. Action items must be concrete and trackable.
Q5: What are the four categories of action items?
A: Prevention (stop repeat), Detection (catch faster), Response (respond faster), Process (better systems/checklists)
