The Incident Commander Role

What an IC Does

  • Coordinates — Owns the war room, directs resources
  • Delegates — Assigns work to specialists (Tech Lead, Comms, etc.)
  • Manages Cognitive Load — Prevents analysis paralysis, time-boxes theories
  • Prevents Hero Culture — No single person burning out, distributes work
  • Makes Decisions — Go/no-go for rollbacks, escalation, communication

Who Should Be IC?

NOT necessarily the most technical person. Should be someone who:

  • Stays calm under pressure
  • Can make quick decisions with incomplete information
  • Knows when to ask for help vs. push forward
  • Has good communication skills

Declaring an Incident

When to Declare

  • Use a low threshold! Better to declare and resolve quickly than miss it
  • Customer-visible impact, even if small
  • SLO being breached
  • Multiple services affected
  • Root cause unclear

Opening Statement (IC's First Words)

13:42 @incident #incident
[IC declares] SEV2: Database is slow, p99 latency 5s. Customer dashboard loads broken. Opening war room. Tech lead investigating schema. Estimated 30min. Updates every 10min.

Actions

  • Create incident in PagerDuty/OpsGenie (severity auto-pages oncall)
  • Create Slack channel: #incident or #sev-X-YYYY-MM-DD
  • Start Google Doc with timeline
  • Page on-call leads in sequence (IC first)

Communication Cadence

Update Frequency by Severity

  • SEV1: Update every 15 minutes (no silence allowed)
  • SEV2: Update every 30 minutes
  • SEV3: Update every 60 minutes

What Goes Where?

  • War Room (#incident): Technical details, theories, decision logs
  • Status Page: Customer-facing, simple language, no jargon
  • Exec Slack Channel: Business impact focused, non-technical
  • Company Slack (#incidents): Brief updates for general awareness

Key Principle

Never go silent. "Still investigating" is better than radio silence. People assume the worst.

Stakeholder Management

Internal Executives

  • Business impact: $X revenue lost
  • Customer count: "~10% of users"
  • Scope: What's down vs. working
  • ETA: Honest, padded timeline

External Customers

  • No technical jargon
  • Honest about issue (avoid fake blame)
  • "We are investigating" vs. "We have identified"
  • Clear ETA or "investigating scope"

Phrase Examples

  • Early Stage: "We are aware of the issue and investigating."
  • Root Cause Found: "We have identified the issue. A database query was slow. We are deploying a fix."
  • Mitigation in Progress: "Fix deployed. Monitoring recovery. Should be fully resolved in 5 minutes."

During the Incident: IC Decision Points

Time-Boxing Theories

  • IC assigns: "Investigate database hypothesis — 15 min max"
  • After 15 min: "Database hypothesis negative. Moving to cache layer next."
  • Prevents: Endless rabbit holes while clock ticks

Parallel Workstreams

  • "You (person A) investigate rollback feasibility. You (person B) check recent deployments. You (person C) check infrastructure metrics."
  • Assign one person per workstream — clear accountability
  • Prevents "too many cooks" chaos

Critical Questions

  • "Can we rollback? How long?"
  • "Can we scale up? Will that help?"
  • "Do we need to degrade a feature to buy time?"
  • "Is this worth a SEV1 escalation?"

Handoffs & Escalation

Clean Handoff Template

[Outgoing IC] Handing off to [Incoming IC].
Summary: Database slow due to missing index on user_id
Status: Cache fix deploying, ETA 5min
Action items: Monitor query performance after deploy
Escalation needed: No, trending down
Next update: 5 min

Rules

  • Never leave mid-incident without handoff. Continuity breaks if you don't.
  • Handoff IC takes 5 min to get context
  • Old IC available for 30 min for questions
  • If incident ongoing > 12 hours, rotate IC every 4 hours

Escalation Matrix

  • SEV1 can't resolve in 30 min → page VP Eng
  • Multi-service outage → page infrastructure lead + service leads
  • Still unresolved after 2h → page CTO

Quiz: Incident Command

Q1: What is the most important quality for an IC?
A: Ability to stay calm and make decisions under uncertainty. Technical skills are secondary — they have Tech Lead for that.
Q2: Why should you time-box incident theories to 15 minutes?
A: Prevents analysis paralysis. Ensures the team moves to the next hypothesis if current one isn't panning out. Speeds up resolution.
Q3: What should an IC NEVER do?
A: Leave mid-incident without a handoff. Blame individuals. Go silent (always communicate, even if "still investigating").
Q4: SEV1 incident. How often should you update the status page?
A: Every 15 minutes. SEV1 requires highest communication frequency. Radio silence is unacceptable.
Q5: How should a customer-facing update differ from an internal war room update?
A: Customer-facing: no jargon, business-focused ("payments affected"). War room: technical details ("database index missing"). Both honest, different audience.
