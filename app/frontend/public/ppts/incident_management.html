Severity Levels

SEV1 - Complete Outage

All customers affected, service completely down. All hands on deck.

Example: Payment processing completely broken, no transactions possible

SEV2 - Major Degradation

Significant impact to customers, service partially down or very slow. Page oncall immediately.

Example: 50% of requests failing, API latency 10x normal

SEV3 - Minor Degradation

Some users impacted, but workarounds exist. Page next morning if during night.

Example: One feature broken but core service works, 5% error rate

SEV4 - Cosmetic / Low Impact

No customer impact or internal-only issue. Create ticket, can wait.

Example: UI button color wrong, typo in error message

Time SLAs Per Severity

  • SEV1: Detect (2min), Triage (5min), Escalate (10min), Resolve (30min-2h)
  • SEV2: Detect (5min), Triage (10min), Escalate (20min), Resolve (2-4h)
  • SEV3: Detect (15min), Triage (30min), Escalate (1h), Resolve (24h)

Incident Roles

Incident Commander (IC)
Owns the war room, makes decisions, ensures communication. NOT necessarily the most technical person. Coordinates the team, manages cognitive load, prevents hero culture. Says "stop digging" when needed.
Communications Lead
Owns status page and stakeholder updates. Non-technical summaries for execs. Manages external customer communication. Never goes silent.
Technical Lead
Investigates and fixes the issue. Reports findings to IC. Proposes remediation steps. Focus on one thing at a time.
Scribe
Documents timeline in real-time. Records decisions and theories tested. This becomes the postmortem narrative.

War Room Best Practices

During the Incident

  • Single Slack channel for sync
  • Video call always on
  • Shared doc (timeline)
  • No blame — systems thinking
  • Time-box theories (max 15min each)

Communication Signals

  • "Investigating path X" — start of theory
  • "Path X negative" — theory ruled out, try next
  • "Stop digging" — too many paths, refocus
  • "Hand off to [person]" — clear ownership transfer
  • "ETA: 30 minutes" — realistic estimate

Communication Templates

Status Page Update (Customer Facing)

"We are aware of degraded performance in the payment service affecting approximately 10% of transactions. Our team is investigating and we'll provide an update in 15 minutes."

Internal Slack Update (All Hands)

What: Payment service returning 500 errors. Impact: 15% of transactions failing. Status: Identified database connection leak. ETA: 45 minutes to deploy fix.

Executive Summary (Leadership)

Business Impact: ~$50K revenue impact in last hour. Scope: Payment processing only, other services unaffected. Resolution: Database fix deploying now, estimated 15min to full recovery.

Incident Tooling Stack

Detection & Alerting

  • Prometheus / Datadog / New Relic: Metrics and alerting
  • PagerDuty / OpsGenie: On-call escalation

Response & Management

  • Slack: Real-time communication channel
  • Google Docs / Notion: Shared incident timeline and decisions
  • Rootly / FireHydrant: Incident command platform (full workflow)

Communication & Tracking

  • Statuspage.io / Atlassian Status: Public incident page
  • Jira: Tracking follow-up action items
  • Confluence / Notion: Postmortem storage and runbooks

Quiz: Incident Management

Q1: What is the role of an Incident Commander?
A: Owns the war room, makes decisions, coordinates the team. Not necessarily the most technical person. Prevents hero culture and manages cognitive load.
Q2: What makes a SEV1 incident different from SEV2?
A: SEV1 is complete outage (all customers affected), requires all hands. SEV2 is major degradation (significant impact), requires immediate page.
Q3: Name three incident roles and their responsibilities.
A: (1) IC: coordinates decisions, (2) Comms Lead: stakeholder updates, (3) Tech Lead: investigates, (4) Scribe: documents timeline
Q4: Why should theories in a war room be time-boxed (max 15 minutes)?
A: Prevents endless investigation of dead ends. Encourages moving to next hypothesis when one doesn't pan out. Speeds up root cause finding.
Q5: What should a status page update NEVER do?
A: Never go silent. Provide updates on a regular cadence. Even "still investigating" is better than no update.
